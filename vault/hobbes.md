# thomas hobbes

![painted portrait of thomas hobbes](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Thomas_Hobbes_(portrait).jpg)
*thomas hobbes — public domain, via [wikimedia commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Thomas_Hobbes_(portrait).jpg)*

> **in one line:** hobbes argues that without a common power to enforce order, human life collapses into a war of everyone against everyone — so rational people would consent to hand near-absolute authority to a single sovereign, trading liberty for security.

## the project

hobbes writes *Leviathan* (1651) in the middle of the english civil war, with the cost of political collapse vividly in front of him. his question is the foundational one in political philosophy: **why should anyone obey a state at all?** his answer starts not from tradition, religion, or divine right, but from a bleak picture of what life would be without any government — and argues that almost any sovereign is better than that.

### the state of nature — a war of all against all

strip away government and what remains? hobbes pictures a **state of nature** with no common authority, and concludes it is a **state of war** — not constant fighting, but the permanent threat of it. his reasoning is structural, not merely pessimistic about human character:

- people are roughly **equal** in ability — even the weakest can kill the strongest by stealth or alliance — so no one is safe by strength alone.
- resources are **scarce** and desires compete, so people clash over the same goods.
- and crucially, with no enforcement, the only rational stance is **pre-emption**: since others might attack, striking first is the safe move, which makes everyone a threat to everyone.

the result is his famous verdict: life without a common power is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." there is no industry, no agriculture, no knowledge — because nothing can be relied upon.

### the social contract — escaping the trap

the way out is mutual agreement. people **covenant with one another** to lay down their natural right to do whatever they please, on condition that everyone else does the same, and to transfer that surrendered power to a sovereign who will enforce the peace. the contract is not between the people and the ruler — it is among the people, who together authorize a sovereign to act on their behalf.

the logic is a trap that only an enforcer can break: each person will keep the peace only if assured everyone else will too, and that assurance requires **a power strong enough to punish defectors.** without the sword, covenants are "but words." the sovereign exists to make agreements reliable.

### the leviathan — why authority must be absolute and undivided

here hobbes draws the conclusion that separates him from later contract theorists. for the sovereign to do its one job — guarantee security — its authority must be **near-absolute and, above all, undivided.** a divided sovereign is a contradiction: if power is split, disputes between the parts have no arbiter, and you slide back toward the war the contract was meant to escape.

this is the direct opposite of [[john-locke]]'s separation of powers and right of revolution. for hobbes, a right to resist the sovereign reintroduces the very anarchy the sovereign was created to prevent. the sovereign is the single point on which the whole order depends — and that concentration is a feature, not a flaw, because the alternative is collapse.

### the mechanistic picture

underneath the politics is a thoroughgoing **materialism.** hobbes treats human beings as matter in motion — appetites and aversions are physical processes, thought is "reckoning," and the commonwealth itself is an "artificial man," a machine assembled from individuals. politics, for him, is a kind of engineering: build the structure correctly and order follows; build it wrong and it tears itself apart.

## where this account falls short

- **the state of nature is a hypothesis, not a history.** humans appear to have always been social; the picture of isolated, pre-social individuals calculating their way into society is a model, and a contestable one — rousseau attacked exactly this.
- **the absolute sovereign has no check.** hobbes builds a cure for anarchy with no remedy for tyranny. once authority is total and irrevocable, nothing protects the governed from the sovereign itself — the single point of order is also a single point of failure.
- **consent is even more fictional than in locke.** no one actually signs the covenant; hobbes leans on tacit consent and on the sheer rationality of submission, which strains to justify obedience to a regime one never chose.
- **the worst-case logic may be too grim.** the pre-emptive-strike argument assumes the bleakest reading of human motivation. cooperation, trust, and reciprocity arise even without a central enforcer more readily than hobbes allows.
