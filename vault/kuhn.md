# thomas kuhn

![portrait photograph of thomas kuhn, 1977](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Thomas_Kuhn_(1977)_(cropped).jpg)
*thomas kuhn in 1977 — public domain, via [wikimedia commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Thomas_Kuhn_(1977)_(cropped).jpg)*

> **in one line:** kuhn argues that science does not advance by steady accumulation but lurches between long stretches of routine "normal science" and rare, disruptive revolutions in which one entire framework is replaced by another wholesale.

## the project

before kuhn, the standard story was that science progresses cumulatively — each generation adds verified facts to a growing pile, marching steadily toward truth. *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962) attacks that picture as a myth, and replaces it with a model where progress is **discontinuous**: punctuated by upheavals in which the basic assumptions themselves change. the book reshaped how we understand not just science but every field with shared frameworks.

### paradigms and normal science

kuhn's central term is the **paradigm**: the whole package a scientific community shares — its accepted theories, exemplary problems and solutions, methods, instruments, and tacit sense of what counts as a good question and a good answer. a paradigm is what a student absorbs by learning to do physics or biology, mostly without it ever being stated.

most science, kuhn says, is **normal science**: not revolutionary discovery but **puzzle-solving within an accepted paradigm.** the framework is taken for granted and not questioned; the work is extending it, refining its measurements, applying it to new cases. this is not a criticism — normal science is where the detailed, cumulative work gets done. but its defining feature is that it does *not* aim to test the paradigm. it assumes it.

### anomalies and crisis

over time, **anomalies** accumulate — results that don't fit, that the paradigm cannot account for. at first these are set aside, blamed on experimental error, or patched with increasingly strained ad hoc adjustments. a few anomalies are tolerated indefinitely. but when they pile up at the paradigm's core, or resist every attempt at resolution, the field enters **crisis**: practitioners lose confidence in the framework itself, and the previously unquestioned assumptions become open to debate for the first time.

### revolution and incommensurability

a **scientific revolution** is the resolution of a crisis by replacing the whole paradigm: ptolemaic astronomy giving way to copernican, newtonian mechanics to relativity and quantum theory. these are not additions to existing knowledge — they redraw the map. the new paradigm changes the basic vocabulary, what questions are legitimate, and what counts as a satisfactory explanation.

kuhn's most contested claim is **incommensurability**: rival paradigms can't be fully compared on a neutral scale, because they don't even share the same meanings for key terms. "mass" means something different to newton than to einstein; the two sides partly talk past each other. there is no theory-independent set of facts both can appeal to in order to settle the dispute cleanly. which is why, kuhn observed, revolutions are won partly by persuasion and generational turnover — "science advances one funeral at a time" — not by decisive proof alone.

## where this account falls short

- **it flirts with relativism.** if paradigms are incommensurable and changes aren't settled by neutral evidence, it can sound like paradigm choice is irrational or merely social, and that later science isn't truly *better* than earlier science. kuhn spent much of his later career resisting this reading and insisting paradigms can be compared on accuracy, scope, and fruitfulness.
- **"paradigm" is notoriously slippery.** one critic counted more than twenty distinct senses in the book. the concept's looseness is part of why it spread so far — and why it is so easy to misuse outside science.
- **it fits some sciences far better than others.** the model of sharp revolutions describes physics and astronomy well, but biology, chemistry, and the social sciences often look more cumulative and pluralistic than the crisis-and-revolution picture suggests.
- **the line between normal and revolutionary is blurry.** real change is often gradual, with overlapping frameworks coexisting, rather than the clean before-and-after switch the model implies.
