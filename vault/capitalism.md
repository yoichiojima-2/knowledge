# capitalism

![posthumous painted portrait of adam smith](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Adam_Smith_The_Muir_portrait.jpg)
*adam smith, the muir portrait, c. 1800 — public domain, via [wikimedia commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Adam_Smith_The_Muir_portrait.jpg)*

> **in one line:** capitalism is a system built around private ownership of productive capacity and a self-reinforcing loop — capital generates surplus, surplus becomes more capital, and whoever starts with the most tends to end with the most.

## what capitalism actually is

capitalism is not simply "markets" or "trade," both of which predate it by millennia. its specific features are: **(1)** the means of production — factories, land, equipment, infrastructure — are privately owned; **(2)** labor is a commodity purchased in a market; **(3)** the goal of deployment is surplus: getting back more than was put in. everything else — prices, competition, wages, finance — is built on top of those three.

### capital — productive capacity you deploy, not just money

capital is **accumulated productive capacity** that can be directed at a task: factories, equipment, trained teams, patents, infrastructure. owning capital means controlling what gets produced and how, not merely having cash. this distinction matters because capital generates more capital — you deploy it to produce surplus, and surplus can be reinvested to produce more.

the capitalist is whoever controls that productive capacity. the worker is whoever owns only their own labor-time and must sell access to it.

### the core loop — M → C → M'

the fundamental unit of capitalist activity is a cycle. money (M) is advanced to buy commodities (C) — raw materials and labor power. these are combined in production and the output is sold for M': more money than started. the surplus — M' minus M — is both the engine's output and its fuel.

the surplus arises from the gap between what workers are paid and the value their labor adds to the output. this gap is structural, not incidental: it is what makes the loop self-sustaining.

### ownership as decision-making power

private ownership of the means of production is the foundational form of authority in a capitalist system. the owner decides what to produce, how, whom to hire, what to pay, and where to reinvest. workers contribute labor and receive wages in return, but they hold no stake in the surplus and no claim on the productive capacity after their shift ends.

this asymmetry is why capitalism and [[neoliberalism]] overlap but are not identical. neoliberalism is an argument about *how to coordinate* (via markets rather than planners). capitalism is an argument about *who owns the productive capacity* and what the loop produces. you can have markets with collective ownership (market [[socialism]]); what makes capitalism distinctive is private ownership combined with the surplus-extraction cycle.

### competition — pressure and its limits

multiple capital-owners competing for the same market creates selection pressure: inefficient firms lose customers, efficient ones grow. profit and loss function as error signals; investment is the response.

but competition tends to undermine itself over time. a winning firm can underprice competitors until they exit, acquire them, or lock in advantages that make new entry impractical. monopoly is not an aberration in capitalism — it is a natural outcome of the accumulation dynamic, because scale advantages are real and compounding.

### accumulation — a loop with no built-in ceiling

because surplus reinvests as capital, ownership concentrates over time. whoever starts with more generates more surplus, which becomes more capital. this positive feedback has no built-in corrective: returns to capital tend to grow faster than the overall economy (what the economist thomas piketty summarized as r > g). the distributional result is not accidental — it is the expected output of the loop when left unconstrained.

the dynamic is amplified by incumbency: existing capital can buy better legal representation, cheaper inputs at volume, and political access that shapes the rules in its favor.

## where this account falls short

- **capital is not easily redeployed.** a factory embeds specific geography, social relationships, and knowledge. "destroying unproductive capital" during a recession means shuttered towns and unemployed families, not just an accounting adjustment. the human cost doesn't appear in the accumulation model.
- **the market signal is lossy.** profit tracks what buyers can *pay*, not what society *needs*. environmental costs, public health, future generations — none of these enter the price unless regulation forces them in. the loop optimizes a proxy; push it hard enough and it diverges from actual welfare.
- **labor is not a commodity like other inputs.** you cannot separate a worker from their labor-time; selling a shift is selling a portion of a human life. the "labor market" framing treats exploitation as a clearing price while erasing what is specific to persons.
- **efficiency doesn't imply equity.** the M-C-M' loop is efficient in the sense of growing capital. but it compounds whatever starting endowment the system begins with. efficient allocation from an unequal starting point reproduces and amplifies that inequality. the model has no native correction for this.
- **the loop produces crises.** overproduction, underconsumption, credit expansion and contraction — capitalism generates recurring crashes that the clean compounding model doesn't predict. losses tend to socialize during crashes; profits accumulate privately during the upswing.
