# david hume

![painted portrait of david hume](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Painting_of_David_Hume.jpg)
*david hume — public domain, via [wikimedia commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Painting_of_David_Hume.jpg)*

> **in one line:** hume pushes empiricism to its breaking point — if every idea must trace back to experience, then causation, the self, and the reliability of induction turn out to be habits of mind we rely on constantly but can never actually justify.

## the project

hume takes a single principle seriously and follows it wherever it leads: **all knowledge comes from experience.** locke and berkeley said this too, but hume is the one willing to accept the unsettling conclusions. in *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739) and the later *Enquiry*, he applies the empiricist rule rigorously and discovers that some of our most basic certainties — cause and effect, the enduring self, the lawfulness of nature — do not survive the test. they are not knowledge in any strict sense; they are habits the mind cannot help forming.

### impressions and ideas — the empiricist test

hume's starting tool is a sorting rule. the mind contains **impressions** (vivid, direct experiences — a color, a pain, a sound) and **ideas** (fainter copies of impressions, recombined in thought). the rule: every genuine idea must be traceable to some original impression. if you cannot find the impression a supposed idea is copied from, the idea is empty — a word with no content behind it. this is hume's diagnostic for exposing concepts that feel solid but rest on nothing experienced.

### causation as habit, not connection

apply the test to **cause and effect** and it fails in a famous way. when one billiard ball strikes another, what do you actually observe? the first ball moves, then the second moves. you see *succession* and *constant conjunction* — one thing reliably following another — but you never observe the necessary connection, the "because," the force that makes the second follow from the first. there is no impression of causal necessity anywhere in the experience.

so where does our unshakable belief in causation come from? **habit.** having seen the conjunction repeated, the mind forms an expectation and projects necessity onto the world. causation, strictly, is a feature of our psychology — a learned expectation — not something we ever find in the objects themselves.

### the problem of induction

this opens onto hume's deepest challenge. all reasoning about matters of fact assumes that **the future will resemble the past** — that the sun will rise tomorrow because it always has, that bread will nourish because it always has. but how is this assumption justified? not by logic: there is no contradiction in imagining the pattern breaking. and not by experience: pointing out that "the future has resembled the past before" already assumes the very principle in question. the justification is circular.

this is the **problem of induction**, and hume's verdict is that there is no rational foundation for it. we generalize from experience because our nature compels us to, not because we have grounds for it. it is the engine of all science and all practical life — and it cannot be justified.

### the bundle theory of self

run the test on the **self** and it fails again. introspect, and you never catch an impression of a continuous "I" — only a perpetual flux of particular perceptions: a sensation, a thought, a feeling, another thought. the self is not a thing you observe underneath these; it is a **bundle of perceptions** in constant change, with no permanent core holding them together. this is the direct opposite of [[descartes]]'s solid, indubitable thinking self — and strikingly close to the buddhist denial of a fixed self.

### the is–ought gap

a final move with long consequences for ethics. hume notices that writers slide, without warning, from describing how things *are* to declaring how things *ought* to be — as if the second followed from the first. but no set of purely factual premises logically entails a value conclusion. **you cannot derive an "ought" from an "is."** facts and values are different kinds of claim, and the gap between them is not bridged by observation alone.

## where this account falls short

- **hume cannot live his own skepticism.** he admits it himself: the doubts dissolve the moment he leaves his study and plays backgammon. a philosophy you must abandon to function may be describing a limit of justification rather than a guide to belief.
- **the problem of induction has no agreed solution, but science works anyway.** the practical success of inductive reasoning is itself a fact in need of explanation. dismissing induction as mere habit undersells how astonishingly reliable that habit turns out to be.
- **the bundle theory struggles to explain unity.** if the self is only a heap of perceptions, what binds them into *one* experiencing stream, and what accounts for memory and the sense of continuity? hume conceded he could not fully account for this.
- **the is–ought gap may be overstated.** some argue that certain facts about suffering or flourishing carry evaluative weight directly, and that the sharp fact/value divide is itself a contestable philosophical position rather than a neutral observation.
