# jean-paul sartre

![portrait photograph of jean-paul sartre, 1965](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Jean_Paul_Sartre_1965.jpg)
*jean-paul sartre in 1965 — cc by-sa 3.0 nl, via [wikimedia commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Jean_Paul_Sartre_1965.jpg)*

> **in one line:** sartre claims that human beings have no given nature or purpose — existence precedes essence — which makes us radically free and therefore wholly responsible for what we make of ourselves, a freedom we constantly try to flee.

## the project

sartre is the central figure of twentieth-century **existentialism**, and his system in *Being and Nothingness* (1943) and the lecture *Existentialism Is a Humanism* (1946) starts from the consequences of [[nietzsche]]'s diagnosis: with no God to design human nature, there is no blueprint for what a person is supposed to be. sartre takes this not as a loss to mourn but as the fundamental fact about human existence — and follows it to a demanding ethics of freedom and responsibility.

### existence precedes essence

a paper-knife has its **essence** before it exists: the artisan has the concept and purpose in mind, then makes the object to fit. for most things, essence comes first. sartre's claim is that for human beings it is reversed: **existence precedes essence.** we first exist — we are simply thrown into the world — and only afterward, through our choices and actions, define what we are. there is no human nature waiting to be discovered or fulfilled, because there was no maker with a design in mind. you are nothing but the sum of what you do; you create your essence by living.

### radical freedom — condemned to be free

if there is no given nature and no external authority to ground values, then at every moment you are **choosing** — your values, your commitments, your very self — with nothing underneath to guarantee the choice. sartre calls this being "**condemned to be free**": freedom is not a privilege you might enjoy but a sentence you cannot escape. even refusing to choose is a choice. even appealing to a rule, a role, or an order is a choice to treat it as binding. there is no situation in which you are not responsible for the stance you take toward it.

### bad faith — fleeing the freedom

this total freedom is experienced as **anguish**, and the natural response is to run from it. sartre's name for that flight is **bad faith** (*mauvaise foi*): lying to yourself to deny your own freedom. his famous example is the waiter who throws himself into the role of "waiter" so completely that he pretends *being a waiter* is simply what he is — as if he were a thing with a fixed essence, rather than a free being who is *choosing* to play the part. bad faith is treating yourself as determined — by your job, your past, your circumstances, your "personality" — in order to avoid the vertigo of admitting you could always do otherwise.

### responsibility and the look

freedom comes with a weight sartre refuses to soften. because you are the author of your choices with no excuses available — no nature, no God, no rule made you do it — you bear **total responsibility** not only for yourself but, sartre argues, for the image of humanity your choices project. and freedom is complicated by other people: under **the look** of another, you become an *object* in their world, fixed and judged from outside. much of human conflict, for sartre, is the struggle between subjects each trying not to be reduced to an object by the other's gaze.

## where this account falls short

- **the freedom is overstated.** to say we are *absolutely* free badly underplays how much biology, poverty, trauma, illness, and social structure constrain real choices. sartre's own later turn toward marxism was in part an attempt to reconcile his philosophy of freedom with the material forces that limit it.
- **"total responsibility" can be cruel.** holding individuals fully accountable for their situation slides easily into blaming the powerless for conditions imposed on them. the framework has little room for genuine victimhood.
- **bad faith may be unavoidable, even necessary.** living in roles and identities is how social life functions; treating all of it as self-deception sets a standard of perpetual authenticity that no one could actually sustain.
- **the ethics is underdetermined.** if all values are freely created and none is grounded, it is hard to see why any choice is *better* than another. sartre insists existentialism is not "anything goes," but grounding that claim within his own system is notoriously difficult.

## related

- [[nietzsche]] — sartre inherits the core problem: how to live and create values once there is no divine source to supply them
- [[descartes]] — both begin from the conscious subject, but sartre denies the subject has any fixed essence to discover
- [[kant]] — a different basis for freedom and responsibility: kant grounds them in universal reason, sartre in groundless choice
- [[philosophy]] — existentialism as the claim that we have no given essence and must define ourselves through action
- [[buddhism]] — a striking contrast: both deny a fixed self, but buddhism aims to release clinging while sartre demands we shoulder radical responsibility
