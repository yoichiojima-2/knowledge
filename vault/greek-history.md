# greek history

![the alexander mosaic, depicting alexander the great at the battle of issus, from the house of the faun in pompeii](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Alexander_the_Great_mosaic.jpg)
*the alexander mosaic (battle of issus), c. 100 bce roman copy of a hellenistic original — public domain, via [wikimedia commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Alexander_the_Great_mosaic.jpg)*

> **in one line:** greek history is the story of how a scatter of small, fiercely independent city-states — too divided to form an empire — produced the political, military, and cultural templates the west kept reusing, and then exported them across three continents the moment a single power finally united them.

## the shape of the story

greek history is usually cut into a handful of eras, and the cuts matter because each one is defined by a different answer to the same question: *how do greeks organize power?*

- **bronze age (c. 1600–1100 bce)** — the mycenaean palace kingdoms, centralized and literate (linear b), the world dimly remembered in homer.
- **dark age (c. 1100–800 bce)** — collapse: the palaces burn, writing is lost, population falls. the slate is wiped.
- **archaic (c. 800–480 bce)** — the *polis* (city-state) emerges as the basic unit; the greek alphabet is adopted; colonies spread across the mediterranean and black sea; sparta and athens take their distinctive shapes.
- **classical (480–323 bce)** — the peak the rest of history quotes: the persian wars, athenian democracy and empire, the peloponnesian war, and the cultural explosion of drama, philosophy, and architecture.
- **hellenistic (323–31 bce)** — after alexander, greek culture becomes the common language of a vast zone from egypt to the indus, ruled by his successors' kingdoms until rome absorbs them.

## the polis: a system of rivals

the defining fact of greek history is that the greeks never became a country. they were a **shared culture spread across hundreds of independent city-states** — common language, gods, and games (the olympics began 776 bce), but no common government. each *polis* was its own sovereign world with its own laws, calendar, coinage, and army.

this produced a permanent tension that drives almost every event below. the cities were similar enough to cooperate against outsiders, and rival enough to never stay united. two of them grew into opposite poles:

- **sparta** — a militarized oligarchy organized around a warrior citizen class supported by a subjugated serf population (the *helots*). its priority was internal control and land power; its culture was discipline, austerity, and the best heavy infantry in greece.
- **athens** — a naval, commercial, and eventually **democratic** *polis*. after the reforms of cleisthenes (c. 508 bce), political decisions were made by an assembly of citizens rather than a king or aristocracy. its power floated on its fleet and its trade.

sparta and athens are not just two cities; they are two answers to how a society should be built, and the classical period is largely the collision between them.

## the persian wars: the cities cooperate

in 490 and again in 480–479 bce, the persian empire — the largest state on earth — invaded greece. the threat did the impossible: it made the cities cooperate, at least briefly. the result was a set of battles that became permanent western reference points:

- **marathon (490 bce)** — athens, almost alone, turns back the first invasion.
- **thermopylae (480 bce)** — a small greek force, spearheaded by spartans, holds a mountain pass to the death, buying time even in defeat.
- **salamis (480 bce)** — the athenian navy destroys the persian fleet in the straits, the decisive blow.
- **plataea (479 bce)** — a combined greek army ends the land invasion.

the wars mattered out of proportion to their size. a coalition of small free states had defeated the era's superpower, and the victory fed a powerful self-understanding — that greek liberty and citizen-soldiers had beaten oriental despotism. that narrative is itself something later europe inherited and romanticized.

## the peloponnesian war: the cities turn on each other

victory created the next crisis. athens converted the anti-persian alliance (the **delian league**) into an empire, collecting tribute and dominating the aegean. sparta and its allies saw a rising power that had to be checked. the result was the **peloponnesian war (431–404 bce)** — a generation-long conflict between the athenian (naval) and spartan (land) blocs that pulled in nearly the whole greek world.

it ended with athens defeated and stripped of its empire, but there was no real winner: the war exhausted everyone, normalized brutality, and left the cities weakened and quarrelsome. thucydides, who chronicled it, treated it as a case study in how power, fear, and self-interest drive states — the founding text of realist political analysis.

## alexander: the export

the cities' mutual exhaustion left an opening on their northern edge. **macedon**, a kingdom the southern greeks regarded as half-barbarian, was unified and militarily modernized under philip ii, who then conquered the squabbling city-states (battle of chaeronea, 338 bce). his son, **alexander the great**, inherited that machine and turned it outward.

in barely a decade (336–323 bce) alexander destroyed the persian empire and carried greek arms to egypt and the edge of india. when he died at 32 his realm fragmented among his generals into the great **hellenistic kingdoms** — ptolemaic egypt, the seleucid east, antigonid macedon. but the cultural result outlasted the political one: greek became the administrative and intellectual *lingua franca* of the eastern mediterranean and near east, cities were founded on the greek model from libya to afghanistan, and alexandria became the scholarly capital of the world. the polis had failed as a political unit but won as a cultural template — which is exactly what rome would later pick up and pass on.

## where this falls short

- **"greece" is an anachronism.** there was no greece, only greeks — hundreds of rival states. narrating it as one nation's story imposes a unity the people themselves never had and would not have recognized.
- **it is athens' story in disguise.** our sources are overwhelmingly athenian, so the classical "golden age" is largely athens' self-portrait. the experience of sparta, the smaller cities, and the persian side survives mostly through their enemies' eyes.
- **the base is invisible.** the leisure, citizenship, and culture rested on slavery and the exclusion of women and foreigners. athenian democracy was direct and radical *for the minority who counted*; most people in any *polis* had no political existence at all.
- **the persian-wars narrative is propaganda as much as history.** "free greeks vs. oriental despots" was a story the victors told and later europe amplified; the persian empire was a sophisticated, tolerant state, and many greeks fought *for* it.
- **the period boundaries are later inventions.** "archaic," "classical," and "hellenistic" are categories imposed by historians, valuing the moments that fed european self-image; people living through them experienced no such clean transitions.
