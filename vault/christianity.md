# christianity

![christ crucified, painted by diego velázquez c. 1632](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Cristo_crucificado.jpg)
*"christ crucified" by diego velázquez, c. 1632 — public domain, via [wikimedia commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Cristo_crucificado.jpg)*

> **in one line:** christianity is a diagnosis and a remedy — human beings are separated from God by a structural fault they cannot repair themselves, and the solution arrives through a mediator who absorbs that fault and makes reconciliation possible through grace rather than personal achievement.

## the core diagnosis

christianity opens with a diagnosis: the relationship between humans and God is broken, and the break is not individual or accidental — it is structural. the theological term is **original sin**: humanity begins in right relationship with God and breaks it, and the break propagates to every subsequent person. it is not a mistake you made; it is a condition you were born into.

this shapes the whole theological architecture, because it means the problem cannot be fixed through individual effort. the fault is in the foundation, not in particular actions. the fix has to address the root.

### the incarnation and atonement — a fix from the inside

the solution is the **incarnation**: God enters human existence as a person — Jesus Christ — who shares the human condition but does not carry the inherited fault. the crucifixion and resurrection are the mechanism: Christ takes on the accumulated debt of human sin, the death is the clearing event, and the resurrection establishes that the mediator was not destroyed by it. through this, reconciliation with God becomes available — not by human achievement, but through what the mediator accomplished.

this is what **grace** means: the gift is given, not earned. the relationship is restored through an act of generosity, not through accumulating sufficient merit.

### grace vs. works — the reformation dispute

the central dispute of the protestant reformation was precisely about how this applies to individuals. is salvation something that must be *achieved* through accumulated good deeds and religious practice? or is it something that is *received* through faith?

the roman church had developed a system where prayers, sacraments, penance, and indulgences all contributed to a person's standing before God — a kind of spiritual credit that could be built up and drawn down. martin luther's reading of paul challenged this directly: justification cannot be earned, only received. the protestant formula — *sola fide* (faith alone) — claims that standing before God is a declaration, not a score computed from accumulated acts. the reformers were not saying works were irrelevant — rather that they could not be the *basis* of salvation.

### the church — community and practice

the church is the community of those who participate in the life made available through Christ. the **sacraments** are practices that enact or maintain that participation: baptism (entry into the community), eucharist (ongoing participation), confession (restoration after failure). the church is also the guardian of **scripture** — the authoritative account of what God has done and what it means.

ongoing debates about sacraments are largely about how directly the individual connects to what Christ accomplished — through institutional practice and mediation, or more directly through faith.

### the trinity — the irreducible claim

the doctrine of the trinity holds that God is one being in three persons: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. every simple model of how this works has been rejected by the church as inadequate:

- "one God playing three roles" (*modalism*) was rejected — the persons are genuinely distinct
- "three separate Gods" (*tritheism*) was rejected — they share one essence

the councils that formulated this doctrine did so precisely because every clean analogy failed. three co-equal, co-eternal, fully distinct persons who are nonetheless fully one God — this was not arrived at for clarity but because every simplification was seen as a distortion. the doctrine resists reduction, and the church has consistently treated that resistance as a feature rather than a problem.

### eschatology — a definite end

christian theology insists that history is not an open-ended process. the world ends deliberately: a second coming, resurrection, and judgment. the goal is not refinement of the present order but a new creation — not a patch to what exists, but a replacement. the present world is real and meaningful, but it is not the final state.

## where this account falls short

- **grace is not a mechanism; it is a relationship.** reducing salvation to an exchange or transaction misses the entire register of love, personhood, and relationship that christianity places at the center. the mediator is not a process — he is a person who *loves*, and the point is not the architecture but the relationship.
- **the trinity will not fit any neat model.** the councils rejected every clean analogy because the doctrine isn't reducible to a structure. treating it as a problem to be solved or a formula to be made sense of misunderstands what the doctrine is doing.
- **works are not irrelevant.** "salvation by grace through faith" can sound like behavior doesn't matter. most christian traditions teach that works are the *fruit* of genuine faith — the outward expression of an inward change — not optional additions. the ethics of love and service are not peripheral.
- **christianity is not one thing.** catholicism, eastern orthodoxy, and the many protestant traditions disagree substantially on authority, sacraments, the nature of grace, and social ethics. any account that treats "christianity" as a single system obscures real and significant diversity.
- **salvation is not only future.** many traditions, especially eastern orthodoxy, understand salvation as *theosis* — ongoing participation in divine life beginning now, not only a verdict to be delivered at the end. flattening this to a future authorization misses the present-tense dimension.
