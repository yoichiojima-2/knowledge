# philosophy

> **in one line:** philosophy examines the assumptions every other discipline takes for granted — asking what exists, what counts as knowledge, what makes reasoning valid, and what we ought to do — and periodically spins off new fields once those questions become answerable by a repeatable method.

## what philosophy does

every field rests on assumptions it doesn't itself test: what counts as evidence, what kinds of things exist, what makes an inference valid. the physicist assumes the external world is real and measurable; the historian assumes testimony can be weighed; the mathematician assumes the rules of inference hold. **philosophy is the discipline that examines those assumptions directly.** it doesn't compete in the same problem space as other fields — it works on the foundations those fields stand on.

its main branches address distinct layers of inquiry:

### what exists — metaphysics

before you can reason about the world, you need a working picture of what's in it. **metaphysics addresses that picture:** what kinds of things exist, how they relate to one another, what causation and time and identity actually are. it's the deepest layer because every other claim presupposes some answer to "what kind of thing are we talking about." most disciplines inherit their metaphysical assumptions silently; metaphysics is where those assumptions get written down and argued over.

### what we can know — epistemology

given a picture of the world, which claims about it should we trust? **epistemology concerns the grounds of knowledge:** what counts as good evidence for a belief, how justified belief differs from lucky guessing, where the boundary runs between knowledge and mere opinion. every research method in every science is, at bottom, a bet on some epistemological position — and when two fields disagree about what counts as evidence at all, they're disagreeing at this level.

### how to reason — logic

**logic concerns the structure of valid inference.** a valid argument is one where, *if* the premises are true, the conclusion must follow — regardless of what the argument is about. logic doesn't tell you whether your premises are true, any more than grammar tells you whether a sentence is honest; it only establishes whether the move from premises to conclusion is legitimate. it applies across every domain of inquiry.

### what to do — ethics and politics

once you have facts about the world, what should be done with them? **ethics asks which actions are permitted, required, or forbidden**, and why. **politics extends this to collective life** — many people, shared resources, competing interests, and the question of what makes authority legitimate rather than merely imposed.

## how fields emerge from philosophy

historically, philosophy has generated new disciplines. physics was "natural philosophy" until it developed reliable experimental methods. psychology, economics, linguistics, and logic (which became computer science) all began inside philosophy. each split off once it developed a stable, independent method — a repeatable procedure it could run on its own.

what stays inside "philosophy" is, by this pattern, the set of questions that *don't yet* (or perhaps *can't*) reduce to a measurable method. this is why philosophy can look like it never makes progress: once a question becomes answerable by a repeatable procedure, we stop calling the work philosophy and give it a new name. the discipline is partly defined by what hasn't yet been handed off.

## where this account falls short

- **the spinoff story makes the residue sound like failure.** "everything answerable left, so what remains is unsolved" implies philosophy is a waiting room for real sciences. but some of its questions — consciousness, meaning, what's genuinely good — may be permanently resistant to empirical methods, not just immature. they might not be bugs waiting for a fix.
- **the foundations are not stable.** you might expect the "base layer" to be settled and uncontested. philosophy's foundations are perpetually reopened — which is the point, not a failure of the discipline.
- **the layers are not cleanly stacked.** ethics can constrain what metaphysics you'll accept; epistemology feeds back into logic. the relationships are bidirectional and tangled, not a clean hierarchy.
- **treating it as infrastructure misses its intrinsic aims.** framing philosophy as "the foundation other fields build on" is useful but reductive. philosophy also has its own aims — the examined life, wisdom, understanding for its own sake — that aren't in service of anything downstream.
